Project Overview

Introduction

India experiences frequent exceedances of surface ozone (O₃), a secondary pollutant formed by photochemical reactions of NOₓ and VOCs under sunlight. Elevated O₃ levels impair respiratory health, reduce crop yields, and exacerbate ecosystem stress. Yet ground‐based monitoring stations remain sparse, limiting spatial coverage—especially in rural and peri-urban regions.

This project develops a high-resolution (0.05° ≈ 5 km) nationwide mapping and forecasting system for both hourly O₃ and daily maximum 8-hour average (MDA8) O₃ by fusing satellite measurements, reanalysis data, and ground observations with advanced machine learning techniques.

Key Objectives
  • High-resolution ozone mapping (0.05°)
  • Uncertainty quantification
  • Model interpretability (SHAP)
  • Operational deployment
  • Policy support applications

Problem Statement

The Surface Ozone Challenge

Surface ozone (O₃) is a secondary pollutant produced when nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) react under sunlight. Unlike stratospheric ozone, ground‐level O₃ harms respiratory health, exacerbates asthma, and reduces agricultural yields by up to 10–20%.

India's rapid urbanization, biogenic emissions, and industrial growth have led to frequent exceedances of the World Health Organization's 8-hour guideline of 100 µg/m³, with limited ground monitoring outside major cities.

Critical Need: High-resolution ozone maps are essential for exposure assessment, public health advisories, and formulating precursor emission control strategies.

Monitoring Gap in India

Current CPCB monitoring stations are concentrated in urban areas, leaving vast rural regions unmonitored.

Health Impacts

Respiratory diseases, asthma exacerbation, premature mortality

Agricultural Loss

10-20% crop yield reduction for major staples

Urban Hotspots

Delhi NCR, Indo-Gangetic Plain, and major metropolitan areas

Literature Review

Comprehensive Literature Analysis

This review synthesizes four key research papers on satellite and machine learning-driven surface ozone modeling, addressing critical gaps in ground-based monitoring data and establishing foundations for high-resolution (0.05°) ozone estimation in India.

Paper 1: CNN-Based Bias Correction for City-Level Ozone Forecasting
Study Overview

Title: "Development of a city-level surface ozone forecasting system using deep learning techniques and air quality model: Application in eastern China"

Region: Eastern China (525 monitoring stations)

Innovation: Hybrid CNN-Physical Model for operational forecasting with 24-96 hour lead times

Methodology
  • Architecture: Weighted AlexNet CNN (11 layers: 5 conv + 3 max-pool + 2 FC)
  • Training: Adam optimizer, MSE with imbalance weighting for high-O₃ samples
  • Data: 525 ground stations (2018-2023), WRF meteorology (9 km), MEIC emissions inventory
  • Hybrid Approach: NAQPMS air quality model provides priors; CNN corrects residuals (δ = observed - prior)
  • Resolution: City-level (~10 km grids)
Key Results
  • RMSE reduced from 18.5 to 13.7 µg/m³ (26% improvement)
  • IOA improved from 0.72 to 0.77 (7% gain)
  • R² = 0.82 for high-O₃ episodes (>100 µg/m³)
  • POD for exceedances = 0.85
  • Outperforms LSTM (RMSE 15.2) and RF (RMSE 16.1)
  • Best performance at 48h lead time (IOA 0.79)
  • Shanghai: RMSE 12.5 µg/m³
  • Beijing: RMSE 14.2 µg/m³
Relevance to Our Project

High Applicability: CNN residual learning directly mirrors our hybrid framework approach.

Adaptation Strategy:

  • Use CPCB stations (similar scale to 525 Chinese sites)
  • Integrate TROPOMI NO₂/HCHO for 0.05° downscaling
  • Apply weighted loss for IGP high-O₃ hotspots
  • Improve POD in data-sparse rural regions
Limitations Noted
  • Compute-intensive training
  • No uncertainty quantification
  • Dependency on emission accuracy
  • Assumes stationary biases
Paper 2: Multispectral Satellite Inference Methodology
Study Overview

Title: "A new methodology for inferring surface ozone from multispectral satellite measurements"

Approach: Transfer function using TES/OMI satellites + TCR-2 reanalysis

Innovation: First satellite-based surface O₃ inference without direct ML training

Methodology
  • Data Sources: TES thermal infrared, OMI UV measurements, TCR-2 reanalysis
  • Transfer Function: Bias-corrected reanalysis ratio applied to satellite columns
  • Validation: TOAR database and MEE ground measurements
  • Resolution: 1.125° (coarse, but global coverage)
  • Temporal: 2005-2010 climatology
Key Results
  • Spatial correlation R = 0.69-0.79 with ground data
  • RMSE: 8.7-13.6 ppb vs. observations
  • Successfully captures monsoon-driven gradients in Asia
  • Identifies NOₓ vs. VOC-limited regimes
  • Asia maps reveal north-south seasonal patterns
  • Bias correction essential (±5-8 ppb reduction)
  • Transfer function stable across years
  • Column-to-surface ratio varies 0.4-0.7
Strengths
  • Global coverage fills monitoring gaps
  • Physical basis ensures interpretability
  • Multi-sensor synergy (thermal + UV)
  • Validated across diverse regions
Our Enhancement

We improve upon this by:

  • Adding ML for nonlinear relationships
  • Using TROPOMI (5.5×3.5 km)
  • Achieving 0.05° vs. 1.125° resolution
  • Incorporating CPCB validation
Paper 3: Long-Term Ozone Trends in India (CMIP6)
Study Overview

Title: "Insights into surface ozone variability in India (1980–2014) through CMIP6 model analysis"

Focus: First comprehensive India-specific O₃ trend analysis using CMIP6 ensemble

Temporal Coverage: 35 years (1980-2014) for climate-chemistry interactions

Methodology
  • Models: 10 CMIP6 ESMs (CESM2, GFDL, etc.) with chemistry modules
  • Validation: AIRS satellite (2° O₃ columns), sparse CPCB pre-2010, hourly post-2010
  • Analysis: Ensemble mean + individual model assessment; monthly MDA8 O₃
  • Resolution: 1-2° grids (physics-based, no ML)
  • Preprocessing: Bias correction via satellite trends
Critical Findings for India
Spatial Patterns
  • North India: +1.2 ppb/decade (NOₓ emission-driven)
  • Central IGP: Negative trends (VOC-limited regimes)
  • South India: Monsoon suppression dominates
  • Urban Hotspots: Delhi NCR, Mumbai, Bangalore
Model Performance
  • Spatial correlation R = 0.75 vs. AIRS (CESM2: R=0.82)
  • RMSE ~10 ppb for MDA8 (CESM2: 8.5 ppb; GFDL: 12.1 ppb)
  • Captures 70-85% of observed variability
  • Underestimates urban peaks by 15-20%
Key Insights
  • Regime Classification: VOC-limited dominant (80% of India area)
  • Monsoon Impact: 30-40% O₃ reduction in southern monsoon season
  • Emission Drivers: North-south NOₓ gradient controls trends
  • Model Limitations: Coarse resolution misses local pollution; VOC/aerosol feedbacks absent
Critical Gaps Identified
  • 1-2° resolution inadequate for policy
  • Sparse ground data pre-2010 limits validation
  • Urban emission uncertainties (±30% for VOCs)
  • No operational forecasting capability
Direct Relevance

Highest relevance - India-specific findings:

  • Use CMIP6 ensemble as physical priors in ML
  • Focus IGP VOC-limited regimes
  • Integrate AIRS→TROPOMI for trends
  • Target 0.05° to address gaps
Paper 4: Machine Learning for Urban Ozone Variability
Study Overview

Title: "Exploring the potential of machine learning for simulations of urban ozone variability"

Location: Doon Valley, India (data-scarce Himalayan foothill region)

Innovation: XGBoost efficiency for operational monitoring in low-infrastructure settings

Methodology
  • Model: XGBoost with grid search hyperparameter tuning
  • Data: ERA-Interim/CAMS reanalysis + in-situ measurements (2015-2018)
  • Features: Meteorology (T, RH, wind) + precursors (NO₂, CO) + temporal (DOY, hour)
  • Target: Noontime O₃ (photochemical peak hours)
  • Training: 70% train / 30% test split; 100 estimators; max_depth=6
Performance Results
  • R² = 0.86 (with meteorology + precursors)
  • RMSE = 6 ppb (best among tested models)
  • Outperforms SVR (R²=0.78) and ANN (R²=0.81)
  • Taylor diagram: Superior phase/amplitude capture
  • Feature importance: T (28%) > NO₂ (24%) > RH (18%)
  • Ablation: Removing precursors drops R² to 0.72
  • Seasonal performance consistent (R²>0.80 all seasons)
  • Training time: 12 min on standard CPU
Key Advantages
  • Efficiency: Fast training for operational updates
  • Interpretability: Feature importance via SHAP
  • Robustness: Handles missing data gracefully
  • Scalability: Proven for nationwide deployment
Implementation Strategy

Primary Model Choice:

  • XGBoost as baseline for CPCB nationwide scaling
  • Integrate TROPOMI NO₂/HCHO
  • Extend to MDA8 O₃
  • Add uncertainty quantification

Develop predictive features informed by literature

Hybrid ML architecture combining strengths from all papers

Data Processing Pipeline
# Sample Python workflow for data integration
import xarray as xr
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from datetime import datetime

# Step 1: Load TROPOMI NO2 data
tropomi = xr.open_dataset('TROPOMI_NO2_20251111.nc')
no2 = tropomi['nitrogendioxide_tropospheric_column']

# Step 2: Load ERA5 meteorology
era5 = xr.open_dataset('ERA5_met_20251111.nc')
temp = era5['t2m'] - 273.15  # Convert to Celsius

# Step 3: Load CPCB ground observations
cpcb = pd.read_csv('CPCB_ozone_hourly.csv', parse_dates=['timestamp'])
cpcb_mda8 = cpcb.groupby(['station_id', 'date'])['ozone'].rolling(8).mean().max()

# Step 4: Spatial regridding to 0.05° target grid
target_grid = {'lat': np.arange(8, 37, 0.05), 'lon': np.arange(68, 98, 0.05)}
no2_regrid = no2.interp(lat=target_grid['lat'], lon=target_grid['lon'])

# Step 5: Feature matrix construction
features = pd.DataFrame({
    'no2_column': no2_regrid.values.flatten(),
    'temperature': temp.values.flatten(),
    'latitude': np.repeat(target_grid['lat'], len(target_grid['lon'])),
    'day_of_year': datetime.now().timetuple().tm_yday
})

# Step 6: Model training (XGBoost)
from xgboost import XGBRegressor
model = XGBRegressor(n_estimators=100, max_depth=6, learning_rate=0.1)
model.fit(features, cpcb_mda8)

# Step 7: Generate predictions
ozone_pred = model.predict(features).reshape(len(target_grid['lat']), -1)

Implementation Details

XGBoost Model Architecture
Hyperparameters (Based on Paper 4)
  • n_estimators: 100 (balanced speed/accuracy)
  • max_depth: 6 (prevents overfitting)
  • learning_rate: 0.1
  • subsample: 0.8 (row sampling)
  • colsample_bytree: 0.8 (feature sampling)
  • objective: reg:squarederror
Feature Importance (Expected)
Temperature (28%)
NO₂ Column (24%)
Humidity (18%)
Solar Radiation (15%)
CNN Residual Correction (Paper 1 Approach)
Architecture
  • Input: XGBoost predictions + errors
  • Conv Layers: 5 layers (filters: 32→64→128→64→32)
  • Kernel Size: 3×3 with padding
  • Activation: ReLU
  • Pooling: MaxPool2D after conv layers 2 & 4
  • Output: Residual correction δ
Training Strategy
  • Loss: MSE with sample weighting (3× for O₃ >100 µg/m³)
  • Optimizer: Adam (lr=0.001)
  • Epochs: 50 with early stopping
  • Final Prediction: O₃ = XGBoost + CNN(δ)
Uncertainty Quantification Framework
Method 1: Quantile Regression
  • Train separate XGBoost models for P10, P50, P90
  • Prediction intervals capture uncertainty
  • Useful for high-O₃ risk assessment
Method 2: Ensemble
  • Bootstrap aggregation (10 models)
  • Standard deviation as uncertainty metric
  • Robust to training data variability
Method 3: SHAP Analysis
  • Explainable AI for feature contributions
  • Regional driver identification
  • Addresses Paper 1 & 4 interpretability gaps
